The Humble Friand

Why I Got Out of Bed at 2AM — Because Some Disasters Won’t Let You Sleep Until You Get Them Right

🍰 Baking, According to Me

They say baking is a science—precise, measured, methodical. But in my kitchen, it’s mostly stubbornness. The kind that keeps you standing barefoot on a freezing tile floor at 5 a.m., swearing at a bag of almond meal that cost more than your electricity bill.

The oven hums like an old friend with secrets, and the recipe card—splattered, dog-eared, and smelling faintly of cinnamon—offers no comfort, only expectations. Somewhere between sifting and swearing, you start to question things. Like why the tart won’t set, why you chose this life, and whether the universe really needed another macaron.

But you keep going. Because sometimes, in the silence between whisks and the soft collapse of a sponge cake, you remember: you’re not just baking. You’re building something. Ritual. Memory. Magic disguised as dessert.

🥄 The Disaster Begins

This is the story of how I completely ruined my first batch of friands, nearly threw in the spatula, and somehow ended up nailing it — with nothing but a plastic jug and pure grit.

It started, as most baking tragedies do, with overconfidence and a suspiciously quiet kitchen. The butter wasn’t melted, the eggs were still cold, and I’d misread the recipe so badly I might as well have been following IKEA instructions for a bookshelf.

At one point, I mistook icing sugar for flour. Twice.

But there was no turning back. I had a mixing bowl full of potential disaster and something to prove — mostly to myself.

❄️ The First Attempt: Muffin Tin Mayhem

I had all the good stuff laid out — butter, icing sugar, almond meal. Wallet cried. Tastebuds cheered. Expectations? Sky high. The result? Absolute garbage. Friands that looked like angry cupcakes and tasted like disappointment dusted in powdered sugar. The batter overflowed like a soap opera subplot. The middles sank like broken promises. Even the muffin tin seemed ashamed. I stared at the mess, spatula in hand, wondering if this was a sign from the pastry gods to pack it in and just buy donuts like a sane person.

I mixed everything — proud, focused, already daydreaming about golden tops and almond-scented applause. Then I waited for the oven to preheat. Big mistake. The batter sat too long. The butter split. Hopes curdled. What came out looked like regret — a crusty brick with a buttery puddle underneath, like it had melted down from the shame of existing. I poked it once, just to see if it would fight back. It didn’t. It just slumped further, like even it knew it had no future.

It was 2AM. I was cold. Tired. Irritated. And worst of all... curious.

☕ The 2:30AM Redemption Bake

I couldn’t let it go. Got out of bed. Winter chill biting at my ankles. No light, no music, no motivation — just unfinished business.

This time, I turned the oven on first — 180°C, like I meant it. Dry ingredients in one bowl. Wet in another. I waited. The oven crept toward temperature while I stood there, silently plotting revenge on that smug little friand tin.

Then it hit me — the revelation: Don’t mix wet and dry until you’re ready to pour. Sounds dumb, I know right...

I grabbed a 2-litre plastic jug. Game-changer. Lined the tins like a pro, like I’d never cried into parchment paper before. Then I folded — gently — the dry into the wet. No whisking. No fury. Just slow, sleepy determination.

Into the jug. Smooth pour into each mould. No mess. No second-guessing. Straight into the oven — like I’d done this a hundred times.

🎉 BOOM. Success.

They came out golden. Light. Moist. Little orange-and-poppyseed pillows of redemption — the kind that make you forget all the disasters before and briefly believe you might just be a genius.

I stared at them like they were newborns. Delicate. Glorious. Slightly uneven but perfect in their own way.

No fancy training. No kitchen magic. Just a bit of patience, a stubborn second wind, and the kind of exhausted joy that makes you want to sit on the floor and whisper, “We made it.”

For the first time that night, I could breathe again. The kitchen smelled like citrus, sugar, and something dangerously close to victory.

And I knew: this batch? This was the one I’d remember.

🧁 The Moral?

You’ll stuff things up. Especially in the kitchen. You’ll burn it. Split it. Waste a whole tray.

But if you get up — even at 2:30AM in the dead of winter — and try again… That’s what makes you a cook.

Not the apron. Not the gadgets. Just that quiet little voice whispering:

“One more go.”

Sometimes, all you need is a bowl, a jug, and a bit of heart. Because the best bakes? They usually come after the worst ones.

I’ve been a cook for 15 years now. But back when this happened? I was still green. Still wide-eyed. And yes… still dumb. The kind of dumb that thinks friands are just fancy muffins and that butter behaves if you just believe hard enough.

But here’s the thing: that catastrophic, 2AM, butter-splitting disaster taught me something I somehow missed in all those years of cooking — If you give up after failure, what was the point of starting?

Because if you burn it, botch it, and bin it... then quit? You’re not just disappointing your oven. You’re disappointing the version of you that believed you could do it in the first place.

And honestly? That version deserves a second shot. Preferably with a plastic jug and a preheated oven.
